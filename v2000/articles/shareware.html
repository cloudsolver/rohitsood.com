The Honesty Factor: Shareware & Freeware
 
Freeware and Shareware basically share the same origin. Prior to the introduction of the IBM PC in 1981, user-groups and BBSs of other computer companies like CP/M machines, Radio Shacks and Apple, passed around software for which the programmer expected less payment. This was because the programs were small and simple, for which support was not on offer, and therefore could not be considered up-market. In 1982, two programmers Andrew Flugleman and Jim Knopf wrote a couple of major applications, videlicet a communications program and a database program respectively on their new IBM PC’s. Not wanting to invest the time and money in trying to get these applications into scores, they decided to take advantage of the distribution networks by allowing their program to be available in abundance.
 
I. Losing Control:
 
Fluegleman called this Freeware (he probably kept the freedom of distribution in mind) and trademarked that name. This was not good for the new software industry; the name Freeware was not quite appropriate since the software was not really intended to be free. As had been done with public domain software distributed in the 1970s. Fluegleman also distributed the source code for his program and lost control over it when dozens of programmers distributed improved versions of Fluegleman’s PC-Talk. History is repeating itself with Linux and it’s n versions. Now, getting back to the 70s…
While Andrew Fluegleman did little to continue to develop and promote PC-Talk, Jim Knopf did a lot more with PC-File and eventually built his database program publishing into a multi million dollar company.
Meanwhile, in 1983, a third programmer, Bob Wallace, wrote a word processing program, PC-Write, which he also developed and promoted into a very successful business. He called it ‘Shareware’. While there were numerous smaller programs and utilities, such as Vern Buerg's wildly popular LIST program, these three major applications were popular with many major businesses, and established the credibility of Freeware as a source of high quality and dependable support (today the views are diametrically opposite). That then paved the way for other, even more successful programs (and tens of thousands less successful programs) to be marked the same way.
       
II. A new name emerges for Freeware
       
In 1984, a column about Freeware and Public domain software was published in a popular computer magazine and a contest to find a new name for "Freeware". While "Freeware" was widely used, it was trademarked and could not be used by many legally. The alternate phrase then was "User Support Software" which was too cumbersome. The most popular choice of the readers' was "Shareware". Incidentally, this was the name; Bob Wallace had given to PC-Write, his word processing program. It became mandatory to ask Mr. Wallace about the authenticity and exclusive claims to the term "Shareware". Wallace had no claims and admitted to have read the name elsewhere, the popular computer magazine then announced the winning name, thus changed the names of "Freeware" to "Shareware".
       
III. Corruption of meanings:
       
As PC-Talk was no longer being distributed, the term "Freeware" lost it's original meaning, in the years that followed it fell into popular usage to mean software for which no shareware fee is charged, obviously such software was not necessarily public domain. Confused?
Although some people refer it to as "Freeware" (and Shareware) as being public domain, the reality is that none of the shareware and very little of the freeware is truly public domain. Since a copyright generally accrues with software that is distributed, for a program to be public domain, the programmer has to specifically label it as such. Freeware then includes some public domain software. Thus the two words seem mutually exclusive and are misunderstood.
       
IV. The History of Shareware Disk Vendors:
       
In early, 1982 a user group called HAL-PC viz. Houston Area League of PC users, cropped up inconspicuously. However, within a year, during 1982 and most of 1983, they tried copious methods of giving copies of shareware library to members. In California, Richard Peterson an obscure programmer obtained a copy of the software library of a local user group and advertised it in PC Magazine for sale. User group members thought this was the equivalent of charging to breathe free air, nevertheless people without access to local user groups welcomed the opportunity to get the software. Peterson called his company PC-SIG. This was the first company to nationally advertise shareware disks for sale.
About the same time, in a shareware column in a computer magazine had prompted many people without access to user groups to write and ask for the programs. When the magazine folded in late 1984, it tried to obtain requests for programs, continued to fill them under the column's name of the Public Library, later adding and distributing legal software written by enthusiasts and hobbyists.
       
V. The Future: Open Source
       
Open-source software is an idea whose time has finally come. For twenty years, it has been building momentum in the technical cultures that built the Internet and the World Wide Web. Now it's breaking out into the commercial world, and that is changing all the rules. The predominant question in the IT industry that is recurrently being asked is whether we are ready for 'open source'
       
VI. History of the Open Source
       
The `open source' label itself came out of a strategy session held on February 3rd 1998 in Palo Alto, California. The people present included Todd Anderson, Chris Peterson (of the Foresight Institute), John `madding' Hall and Larry Augustine (both of Linux International), Sam Ockman (of the Silicon Valley Linux User's Group), and Eric Raymond. They were reacting to the Netscape's announcement that it planned to give away the source of its browser. One of them (Raymond) had been invited out by Netscape to help them plan the release and follow actions. They realized that the Netscape announcement had created a precious window of time within which they might finally be able to get the corporate world to listen to what they have to teach about the superiority of an open development process.
They realized it was time to dump the confrontational attitude that has been associated with `free software' in the past and sell the idea strictly on the same pragmatic, business-case grounds that motivated Netscape. They brainstormed about tactics and a new label. `Open source', contributed by Chris Peterson, was the best thing they came up with.
       
VII. Shareware and the Net: Freedom at last
       
With the advent of the Internet, programmers all over the world saw the infrastructure as an ideal means through, which their programs could reach millions of people. This was the ultimate global freedom, a unique platform where the top computer firms and individual programmers, hobbyists and free-lancers had an equal opportunity to reach out to the masses in the same way.
The size of the net, today, cannot be accurately determined, but Internet statisticians, however, estimate that there are over 7000 lakh web pages contained in over 20 lakh web sites on the internet today. The size of the Internet is increasing at a phenomenal rate. By the time one finishes reading this paragraph one more web site would have been hosted on the net, and thousands (millions?) upgraded. This only goes to show that the content on the World Wide Web defies the imagination.
Software is the best selling item on the net, but the erudite consumer does not buy it unless the utility of the software can be verified, and therefore will make an assessment of the software before the purchase. The prospective consumer usually downloads the “Trial” software or shareware from the source, to run it on his computer for a stipulated period of time. The user might want to share this software with his contacts for assessment.
A "shareware" program is not free; it is only that creators are willing to let the prospective consumer use their products in order to test it. Shareware may be shared with anyone free of cost; it can be used for a certain amount of time and must either be purchased or deleted from the prospective user's computer.
The author of the shareware program retains the right to the program, but allows it to be sold or to be distributed. The idea of the shareware is based on an honor system. It is the prerogative of the consumer that he passes on the shareware to others for assessment. He may, or may not accept any payment in return, and he may not alter in any way the shareware. Shareware products (i.e. software advertised and perhaps purchased in this way e.g. WinZip) are almost always cheaper than comparable commercial software (which are advertised on various media, since the shareware version of the software is not on release, e.g. Microsoft Office); it is now being recognized that it is in everyone's interest to support shareware or freeware.
The software the prospective consumer uses for assessment is called freeware or shareware. The term "free" in Freeware refers to the freedom of distribution and must not be associated with its cost or copyright. It is not free software. In that case, isn’t Shareware and Freeware the same thing? Well, it is, almost. To put it flatly, Freeware is a copyright name for Shareware and is grossly misused today for Public Domain software..
Software downloaded from any web site free of cost unusually is shareware, open-source software, or public domain software. One is required to pay for the mentioned categories of software except the public domain software. Off late many programmers have categorized their programs "Careware", or "Postcard ware" etc. If you want to use a Careware program you must promise to be respectful and caring for the elders. Postcardware, as the name suggests require you to send a postcard to the programmer if you like the program. The list is creative, but endless.
       
VIII. The Principle
       
Shareware and freeware is software distributed based on an honor system. Shareware is inexpensive because it is usually produced by a single programmer and is offered directly. Thus, there are practically no packaging or advertising expenses.
Shareware is a distribution system for computer software based on the "try-before-you-buy" principle. Shareware is NOT free. Shareware authors are generally protected by various organizations. The Compact Disc being clearly the most economical, convenient and mutually beneficial data storage media has gained a lot of popularity worldwide and is catching on in India as well.
       
IX. The Method of distribution
       
Shareware is collectively a type of software and a means of distribution of that software. It is a program written for distribution. The modes of distribution are mainly the Internet and the CDs. The latter gaining over the other as far as economics are concerned.
       
X. The Cost and Honesty Factor
       
Shareware works largely on the honesty factor in people. It relies mainly on the conscience of the individual to eventually do the right thing and order the product to continue use of the program. This honesty factor is fully capitalized by Microsoft, which as a company policy does not protect its software from piracy (it is fairly simple to put a CD copy lock), thus hoping that the prospective consumers buy the product after using the pirated copy. They hope that the user will appreciate the difference between a pirated copy and the benefits. Though, this approach is not shareware, it is effective nonetheless. However the shareware method completes a trust that the author initiates releasing the program via the shareware method. The author's success is not only dependent on the usefulness of the program and it's distribution, but the conscience of the person using it.
The program's documentation tells users the registration amount and how to send it. Registrations can be sent in many forms. The service usually takes a percentage of the registration in trade for collecting the registration and keeping records
The programmers send their shareware to CD-ROM publishers and shareware disk vendors. In most cases, it is reviewed by the system operator of site management, and then posted along with a short description, for user downloading. Also the C.D. ROM publisher accumulates lots of shareware programs and sells it to the end consumer.
Although the actual registration to download ratio has a variety of factors, extensive distribution is the key to obtaining the highest number of registrations. As a rule, the more people download it, the more people will register it. Most shareware authors begin by uploading their program all over the place, wherever they can. This can take hours, days, even weeks. Some shareware authors give their programs to shareware CD writers for distribution. A variety of places offer distribution of an author's shareware program. Some services overlap, some do not, some are free, some are not, some create CD-ROMs, some do not. As a thumb rule, in India a shareware CD-ROM rather than direct Internet uploads are the best bet for upcoming programmers to reach out to the Indian masses with their work. Besides saving a lot of time, energy and money, it is efficient, effective and mutually beneficial. It is, therefore in our interest to support shareware, and more so on CD ROMs (DVDs in the near future?), until the connectivity speeds and the costs of Internet time reduces.
