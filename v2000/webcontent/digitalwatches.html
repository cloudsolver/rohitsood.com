Digital Watches and the Internet
 
As you know, I'm prone to reminisce. As long as you don't make your home
permanently in the past, there's nothing wrong with occasionally going back and
slipping a memory on like a comfortable old shoe. Today, we hearken back to my
first digital watch.


The year was 1975. A fellow by the name of Ford was the President, and every
Saturday night I looked forward to watching Chevy Chase imitate his trips and
falls (that the news camera always seemed to catch). The Big Red Machine was
still running. Disco hadn't yet reared its ugly head. Polyester was fashionably
hot. The world was running out of oil. 8-track vs. cassette was a debatable
subject. Many of us prayed for a Beatles reunion. I was working my first "real"
job, sacking groceries for $2.10 an hour at Phillip's Grocery in Pea Ridge,
Arkansas.


It was under such circumstances that I obtained my first LED digital watch. 
The wristwatch of the day didn't bear much resemblance to what we wear now.
Bulova made a watch called the "Accutron" that ran on a battery. It was pretty
radical (and expensive). Most of us poor folks either wound our Timexes manually
or else owned "self winders." Self winding watches had a small eccentrically
weighted wheel that constantly turned as a result of normal wrist motion and
kept the mainspring wound. Your watch ran as long as you wore it, but would
eventually stop if you took it off for long periods of time. 


So you can imagine the amazement that we all felt when the digital watch sprang
upon the scene. This incredible little piece of electronics presented a dark
face that showed the time when a button was pushed. The information was
presented via actual numerals, ignoring the paradigm of a dial and hands.
As I recall, in '75 you could obtain one of these technological marvels for
around 150 bucks. Battery life was directly affected by the frequency of
checking the time. 

New owners would look approximately every thirty seconds.
This meant that the watch, guaranteed to be accurate to within a minute per
year, would need to be shut down and reset about once every two months. Once you
got past the novelty and settled into a routine of seeing what time it was
perhaps ten times per day, then a battery might possibly last five months. 
To illustrate the technological jump that was made with this watch, picture a
thirty-dollar Timex of the era. It was pretty bare-basic, no calendar or
waterproof feature at that price. 

A local realty advertised by giving away twelve little aluminum calendars with bendable fingers that wrapped around your watchband, one per month. That way you could get the time and the date with one glance at your wrist. The watch's most telling feature was located inside. If
you popped open the case, you found an adjustment marked "+" and "-." This
allowed you to slow or speed the works in an effort to make the darned thing
accurate to within, say, five minutes per month. If you managed to make it keep
decent time, you held on to it for dear life. The adjustment process might take
weeks to fine tune

.
So now, you could purchase a timekeeping device that did not vary by more than a
minute in a year (or 25 seconds within the maximum five-month battery life).
Simply astounding. 

I was the proud owner of one of these babies. I used to blow the English class
away with my ability to count down to the exact second that the period would
end. I spent a few hours in detention for interrupting the teacher's soliloquies
with "54321" countdowns that climaxed with that familiar ringing sound. Oh
well, it was worth it.

Then, one day, I was stacking bags of sugar in the back of the store. I had
taken the watch off, not wanting to damage my 150-dollar investment. When we
finished unloading the truck, I took a break, leaving my watch on a shelf in the
loading dock. The truck driver helped himself to it and "split," as we used to
say.I reported the watch's theft to the local police, who showed up and took a
detailed report. Needless to say, I never saw it again. I was sick for weeks.
This brings us to today. LCD digital watches are routinely given away as
advertising tokens. Battery life: five years. If one gets stolen, you just buy a
twelve-pack of pop or the like and get another one for free. They haven't made
LED's since the 70's. Digital watches, far from carrying the status that they
once held, are now viewed as strictly casual wear. No self-respecting
businessman would be caught dead wearing one with a Hart, Shaffner, and Marx
suit. How times change.

One day, you'll be telling your kids and grandkids what it was like in the early
days of the internet. They will gaze at you wonderingly as you relate what it
was like to connect through a telephone line at 28,800 BPS, to manually
configure Windows 95 dialup networking, or maybe to set up Winsock on a 16-bit
system. Just don't forget to explain what a "modem" was. They'll have no idea.
