The Tennis Service and the Realization of C.I.
       
I have been playing tennis for the past 15 years, and it was only in the past 15 days that I realized that all the facets of the game reflected what was taught by Maharishi Mahesh Yogi in 1972. I found that all principles of Creative Intelligence and analogies explained during the course had a deep-rooted relationship with every stroke of the game of tennis. For instance, the Bow and Arrow analogy, the Strawberry analogy and the Principle of Least Action apply to the game. In fact, they apply to each stroke in the game of tennis, so I picked out the serve in lawn tennis and analyzed it. Here are the results that surprised me.
I love the game and any player would know that the serve in this game is the most critical part, it can decide the result of most matches, and it can give us a good edge and make us a winner. In the game of life, the realization of Creative Intelligence is very critical; the practice of Transcendental Meditation is as critical as the toss of the ball just before the serve
LOOKING DEEPER
The serve for me is a simple stroke, but when I looked deeper into the mechanics involved I realized that it was analogous to the principle that the Bow and Arrow analogy illustrated. I toss the ball up into the air and hit it. However, there is a special moment when I must hit the ball for the serve to be the most powerful and effective, i.e. when the ball stops in mid-air. That moment is similar to the situation when the arrow is pulled back before it shoots out with maximum energy. Just in the same way I see that in TM I try to put my mind to a complete state of rest by sinking into myself and finding the Unified Field. I, therefore, conclude that it is the complete state of rest in the tennis ball that allows it to move with greater speed when struck by the racquet as with the mind. So the rest and action cycle is true for a tennis serve as explained by Maharishi, but he used the bow and arrow analogy for the same.
I was surprised to find that the Strawberry Analogy has a relationship with the serve. As explained in the SCI course, when one tastes a strawberry, one’s natural tendency is to eat it in many different ways to capture the entire depth of its taste. This intrinsic desire to learn more looks for immediately fulfillment. I had the same desire for my tennis serve. This tempted me to explore deeper into the knowledge of the tennis sever; I was naturally prompted to learn more and therefore started experimenting with the stroke. Listening about the strawberry analogy and that the point that Creative Intelligence naturally promotes deeper knowledge rang a bell. It was CI at work when I felt like learning more about the stroke. This was a revelation.
Creative Intelligence had naturally prompted me to know more and more, deeper and deeper about the subject in question: the tennis serve. At first I had learnt the cannon ball or the flat serve. I then started to explore other varieties of the tennis serve. I started to try out the sidespin serve, topspin and the kick-up serve using other hybrid grips. It was so natural, but I had never thought that it was the nature of Creative Intelligence that was behind this.
MINIMUM EFFORT MAXIMUM BENEFIT
Finally, The Principle of Least Action and the Sweet Spot Principle of tennis are parallel to each other. I had always wondered what the sweet spot was. During my younger days I would always wonder why tennis coaches talk so much about the sweet spot and whether my taste buds had anything to do with it. I learnt in one of the coaching sessions that the “sweet spot” is located at the center of my tennis racquet and not the tongue as I had imagined earlier. In a serve when the ball strikes the spot the stroke will be the fastest and effortless too. This illustrates the Principle of Least Action. When we serve properly, we hit the sweet spot, minimum effort is then required to get the maximum benefit.
The Science of Creative Intelligence is omnipresent, it is a universal science and I have seen how it has applied to something I couldn’t have imagined it would ever: the serve in lawn tennis. I am an internationally recognized tennis coach, and I plan to teach a little SCI along with the standard tennis lessons so that my students can get the complete fullness from each lesson they learn from me. I hope each of us promote this wonderful knowledge in as many flavors and styles as they possibly can so that one day we will have everyone beaming in glory of this knowledge in a peaceful world.
 
-Rohit Sood
